About

I've never really known how to answer the question, "What do you do?" Every time I think I've found one answer, life hands me another.

I grew up watching my family's jewellery business up close, long before I knew words like branding or strategy. What I noticed instead was simpler — why a customer kept coming back, what made someone trust a shopkeeper they'd known for twenty years. I didn't know it then, but that's probably where my curiosity about people, and about businesses, actually started.

I studied International Business because I wanted to understand how ideas travel across borders. But college became so much more than lectures and exams — it became a place to try on different versions of myself. A semester spent helping build a race car with a Formula Student team. A year spent as CSR Head, mostly learning that leadership is quieter than it looks, and happens long before anyone's clapping.

I kept saying yes to things that felt just outside my comfort zone — volunteering, anchoring, organising, competing — mostly because I was curious what would happen if I did.

Swimming taught me discipline. Events taught me people. Business taught me systems. Community taught me empathy.

These days I work as a property consultant in Pune, and alongside that, I keep a hand in my family's jewellery business while building consumer brands through ProspAura, including ONYU. But if you ask me what I do, none of those titles feel like the whole answer. Curiosity feels closer to the truth.

Lately I've been falling down rabbit holes about physics and philosophy — not because I understand most of it, but because the questions underneath (why does time feel the way it does, why does anything exist at all) refuse to leave me alone. It's the same itch that pulls me into consumer psychology, or has me noticing why one building makes you want to linger and another makes you want to leave.

I'm the type of person who'll spend one evening reading about behavioural psychology and the next learning to cook something I've never attempted. I'll cross a city because someone mentioned a café. Stand beneath the Acropolis wondering how an idea outlives the person who had it. I don't think I'm Type A, and I don't think I'm Type B — I'm ambitious without wanting my whole life to revolve around work, and I care more about people and ideas than I do about most things with my name on them.

I bake well, cook, mostly when I'm happy. I journal when I'm confused. I travel whenever I can, and somewhere along the way I've learned that the best education rarely comes from a classroom. It comes from conversations, train stations, wrong turns, unexpected friendships.

I've learned to enjoy my own company. I've become the person I can rely on most. And I hope I never stop becoming.

Before all of this

Business wasn't something I studied before I lived it.

I grew up in an entrepreneurial family, and long before I understood terms like margin or brand positioning, I understood people, who trusted, why a customer came back, what made someone feel taken care of. Business, to me, was a thing that happened over dinner conversations, not something in a textbook.

Nobody in my house believed in one fixed path. I was encouraged to explore a new hobby, a new city, a new interest without needing much of a reason beyond wanting to. And somewhere along the way, I was trusted with real responsibility earlier than I probably expected: decisions, money, figuring things out without someone hovering nearby.

I think that's genuinely where my independence comes from not one dramatic moment, but a hundred small ones. My parents quietly stepping back so I could step forward.

It's probably why trying something unfamiliar has never scared me quite as much as it maybe should.

Curiosity

Ask me about physics, human behaviour, why a certain café feels good to sit in, or why some cities feel alive and others don't — I'll happily lose an hour to it. Consumer psychology, branding, architecture, design, cities, businesses, and the strange overlap between physics and philosophy are all, somehow, the same question to me: why do things — and people, and places — work the way they do. I don't think I collect answers so much as I collect better questions.

Reading

Reading is one of my favourite forms of solitude. Left alone with a book, I'm genuinely happy. Most of my shelf is philosophy, psychology, behavioural science, business, and biographies — books that quietly rearrange how I think about something. But I have guilty pleasures too: fiction, comfort reads, and the occasional novel I finish in one sitting because I refuse to put it down. How I actually fell in love with reading is a story for another day.

Cooking

I genuinely love cooking — my friends are always a little surprised by how much. I like experimenting with cuisines and techniques I've never tried, understanding why a dish works rather than just following the steps. Taking a pasta class from a Michelin-starred Italian chef is a pretty good example of how I like to learn things: hands in the dough, not from a book.

Travelling

I've backpacked through northern and southern India and realised that travelling isn't about collecting destinations — it's about collecting perspectives. Every city teaches me something different, whether it's resilience, patience, culture, food, or simply slowing down long enough to notice life. (More on this on the Travels page.)

Swimming

Swimming has been one of the longest relationships in my life. It taught me consistency before motivation, discipline before results, and that progress usually happens quietly, and it's just genuinely fun.

Dancing

Some nights are meant for music, crowded dance floors, and laughing until 2 a.m. I love celebrating life just as much as I love reflecting on it usually within twelve hours of each other.

Solitude

I genuinely love my own company. I don't need constant stimulation, or people around me, to feel like a day was well spent. Some of my happiest days are just reading, writing, cooking, walking, or listening to music with absolutely no agenda. Solitude is where I recharge, ikr sounds odd for one of the most outgoing extroverted person you'd meet but honestly it's, where most of my ideas begin, long before they're ever said out loud.

People

I genuinely love meeting people. Not because of networking, but because every conversation teaches me something I couldn't have learned alone.

Philosophy

I'm fascinated by questions people have been asking for centuries — meaning, purpose, identity — and lately, the strange overlap between physics and philosophy. What does it actually mean that time might not move the way we experience it? I find myself happily stuck somewhere between the two.

Design

I notice fonts, interiors, packaging, the way a lobby makes you feel the second you walk in. Branding, consumer psychology, architecture — different names, I think, for the same question I can't stop asking: why do certain things, and places, make us feel a certain way?

Community

Community has shaped me since I was fifteen.

LMAD became one of the biggest influences in my life. Over the years I've volunteered, organised conferences, facilitated youth discussions, mentored younger students, coordinated teams, planned events, managed logistics, and learned that service doesn't always have to be loud.

During university I continued that through student leadership — CSR Head, large-scale campus events, anchoring, volunteer initiatives. Mostly, I was just trying to bring people together and create spaces where they felt seen.

Impact isn't always measured by numbers. Sometimes it's measured by one conversation.

One opportunity. One person who leaves believing a little more in themselves.

Perspective, mostly

I don't travel to collect countries.

I've been travelling on my own, in some form, since before I really understood what independence meant.

Since around third grade, my parents started sending me to summer and winter camps without them — on purpose. They wanted me comfortable being somewhere unfamiliar, meeting people I didn't know, figuring things out without someone to run to. At the time it just felt like camp. Looking back, I think it's the whole reason solo travel has always felt natural to me instead of intimidating.

That early practice at being okay in unfamiliar places turned into an actual curiosity about them — different cultures, different people, the small things a place teaches you that no guidebook can.

I travel to collect perspectives, not destinations.

Athens taught me that ideas can outlive generations. Italy reminded me that craftsmanship deserves patience. Northern India taught me resilience. Southern India taught me slowness.

Every journey has left me thinking a little differently than the one before it.

Where this is all going

I don't want one career.

I don't want one company. I don't even want one definition.

I hope to spend my life building across different worlds. Businesses that solve meaningful problems. Communities that help people grow. Places where creativity feels safe. Products that improve everyday life. Spaces that bring strangers together. Ideas that make someone pause and think.

I want to invest in people. Mentor. Learn. Travel. Create. Keep changing my mind. Keep beginning again.

I hope that decades from now people won't remember me for one company. I hope they'll remember that I built things worth caring about.
